Biography

Early Life

Sabrina Annlynn Carpenter was born on May 11, 1999, in Quakertown, Pennsylvania, to David and Elizabeth Carpenter, and grew up in East Greenville. She was homeschooled and has three older sisters. Carpenter is also the niece of actress Nancy Cartwright. Around the age of 10, she began uploading videos to YouTube, covering songs by artists like Christina Aguilera and Adele. Recognizing her passion for music, her father built a recording studio at home to support her dreams. In 2009, she placed third in The Next Miley Cyrus Project, a singing competition organized by Miley Cyrus.

Career at Disney

In January 2013, Sabrina Carpenter was cast as Maya Hart in the Disney Channel series Girl Meets World, a spin-off of Boy Meets World. The show ran for 72 episodes and concluded on January 20, 2017. Carpenter also recorded the series’ theme song with her co-star Rowan Blanchard. Before signing a five-album deal with Disney’s Hollywood Records in 2014, she had released several independent promotional singles between 2010 and 2013.

Early in her career, Sabrina Carpenter was often labeled a “teen pop singer.” As her sound evolved, she leaned more fully into pop music. Barry Pierce of I.D. remarked that following the release of Emails I Can’t Send, Carpenter had earned the title of a “fully fledged popstar.” Thania Garcia of Variety observed that although Carpenter was long seen as a “Disney princess,” her shift from child actor to pop artist has been “slow, steady, and intentional.”

Transition to Music

Carpenter’s debut album, Eyes Wide Open, was released on April 8, 2015. The album debuted at number 43 on the Billboard 200 and was certified Gold by the RIAA. Her second album, EVOLution, was released on October 14, 2016, and debuted at number 28 on the Billboard 200. Carpenter’s third studio album, Singular: Act I, was released on November 9, 2018, and debuted at number 23 on the Billboard 200. The follow-up album, Singular: Act II, was released on July 19, 2019.

Carpenter herself admitted that moving on from her Disney image was challenging, though she has since gained greater creative control over her work—a point Pierce also emphasized. In an interview with Vogue, Carpenter reflected that her earlier music represented only a part of her identity and didn’t feel entirely genuine at the time. Her dynamic stage presence has also drawn praise, with Vogue writer Chelsea Sarabia describing her as an artist who “wields the full range of human expression as if it were an instrument of its own.”
